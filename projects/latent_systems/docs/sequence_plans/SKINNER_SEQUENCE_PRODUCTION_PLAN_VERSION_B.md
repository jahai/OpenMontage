# Claude Handoff: B. F. Skinner Harvard Sequence, Version B

## How to use this file

This is a production handoff for continuing the B. F. Skinner documentary sequence in Claude or another writing/editing system. The locked direction is **Version B: narrator voiceover, sparse factual subtitles, and one or two verified Skinner quote cards**. Do not invent dialogue for Skinner. Any words attributed to Skinner must be exact quotes from a primary or authoritative source, with page number, archive identifier, or stable URL.

The creative target is a documentary-accurate Skinner sequence that borrows the structural grammar of Christopher Nolan’s *Oppenheimer* opening without copying protected dialogue or staging. The relevant reference video is the *New York Times* “Anatomy of a Scene” for *Oppenheimer*, where Nolan discusses raindrops as motif, Trinity imagery, close camera pressure on Oppenheimer, black-and-white timeline separation, and the contrast between public grandeur and claustrophobic hearing-room pressure ([NYT Anatomy of a Scene](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=o_GPSEVEasE)).

## Project goal

Create a visually rigorous documentary sequence about B. F. Skinner’s Harvard years from 1928 to 1936, especially the period when he developed the early operant chamber, rate recorder/cumulative record, and foundational rat research. The sequence must read as historically grounded rather than generic “1930s lab” cinema. It should connect the Skinner loop to modern infinite scroll and device addiction through theme and visual analogy, not by claiming Skinner invented modern apps or social media.

## Locked editorial direction

Use **Version B**:

- **Narrator voiceover**: carries theme, continuity, and the broader moral question.
- **Subtitles**: establish date, place, and conceptual anchors.
- **Skinner quote cards**: only exact verified quotations from primary or authoritative sources.
- **Modern phone shots**: interpretive splice-ins, not historical claims.
- **No invented Skinner dialogue**: Skinner can be silent, observational, and physically present. If he speaks, use sourced words only.

Recommended rough-cut test: assemble without actor dialogue first. Add one or two Skinner quote cards only after the exact quotations have been sourced and logged.

## Core visual thesis

The Skinner box should function in this sequence the way the atom functions in *Oppenheimer*: not merely as an invention, but as a visual-moral device. The central cinematic unit is the loop:

1. A rat presses a lever.
2. A pellet falls.
3. A recorder line moves.
4. The action repeats.
5. The visual grammar later rhymes with thumb, tap, notification, scroll, and engagement curve.

The sequence should not say “Skinner invented infinite scroll.” It should say something more precise: the same behavioral vocabulary of response, consequence, reinforcement, repetition, and measurable behavior later becomes central to persuasive interface design.

## Historical source baseline

### Skinner timeline

Skinner entered Harvard graduate study in 1928 at age 24, received his Ph.D. from Harvard in 1931, remained as a researcher until 1936, held a National Research Council Fellowship from 1931 to 1933, and was a Harvard Society of Fellows Junior Fellow in psychology from 1933 to 1936 ([B. F. Skinner Foundation Operants](https://www.bfskinner.org/wp-content/uploads/2016/02/OPERANTS_1_2016.pdf), [Harvard Psychology](https://psychology.fas.harvard.edu/people/b-f-skinner), [Harvard Society of Fellows](https://socfell.fas.harvard.edu/past-junior-fellows-psychology)). Skinner left Harvard for the University of Minnesota in 1936 and did not return as a Harvard professor until 1948, so Memorial Hall pigeon-lab imagery belongs to a later period rather than the 1928-1936 Harvard rat-work sequence ([Harvard Psychology](https://psychology.fas.harvard.edu/people/b-f-skinner), [Harvard CHSI](https://chsi.harvard.edu/in-the-lab)).

### Skinner appearance and clothing

Render Skinner as a slim young adult, clean-shaven, with blue eyes and sandy hair that could appear awry. Bjork’s biography describes the 24-year-old Skinner as slim, blue-eyed, and sandy-haired, and records a classmate’s impression of him as thin but wiry and quick in movement, speech, and thought ([Bjork 1993](https://gwern.net/doc/psychology/writing/1993-bjork-skinneralife.pdf)). A Skinner letter cited by Bjork gives his weight at roughly 140 lb around 1930, which supports a lean visual build rather than a heavy one ([Bjork 1993](https://gwern.net/doc/psychology/writing/1993-bjork-skinneralife.pdf)).

Use dark suit jacket or sport coat, white shirt, and dark tie as the safest period look. The April 1933 Bettmann/Getty portrait identifies Skinner as one of the Harvard men selected for the new “Super school,” meaning the Society of Fellows, and provides a period-correct face, hair, and clothing reference, though it is not a laboratory photograph ([Getty/Bettmann](https://www.gettyimages.com/detail/news-photo/skinner-is-one-of-the-students-chosen-by-harvard-to-become-news-photo/514866492)). Fred Keller’s description of the graduate-student era as stylish and elegant supports a dressed academic look, not a default white-lab-coat image ([Bjork 1993](https://gwern.net/doc/psychology/writing/1993-bjork-skinneralife.pdf)).

Do not put glasses on Skinner for the 1928-1936 sequence unless a newly discovered period source requires it. The known 1931 and 1933 period portrait leads do not support glasses, while later images such as the circa-1950 Harvard portrait do show glasses and therefore should not be backdated ([B. F. Skinner Foundation Operants](https://www.bfskinner.org/wp-content/uploads/2016/02/OPERANTS_1_2016.pdf), [Getty/Bettmann](https://www.gettyimages.com/detail/news-photo/skinner-is-one-of-the-students-chosen-by-harvard-to-become-news-photo/514866492), [Wikimedia Commons](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:B.F._Skinner_at_Harvard_circa_1950.jpg)).

### Harvard lab setting

The 1928-1936 setting should not be reduced to either “psychology Emerson Hall” or “Crozier physiology lab.” The source-backed resolution is chronological and institutional: Skinner entered Harvard in the Department of Philosophy and Psychology, was described in December 1930 and February 1931 as working or being “at present” in the Psychological Laboratory, and then moved into National Research Council / General Physiology sponsorship under W. J. Crozier beginning with the 1931-1932 fellowship ([B. F. Skinner Foundation Operants](https://www.bfskinner.org/wp-content/uploads/2016/02/OPERANTS_1_2016.pdf), [Harvard Psychology](https://psychology.fas.harvard.edu/people/b-f-skinner)). Crozier’s own correspondence described Skinner before the fellowship as “primarily working in the Psychological Laboratory and only secondarily” in connection with General Physiology, which means the psychology-versus-physiology split is not a clean building substitution but a shift in sponsorship and intellectual home ([B. F. Skinner Foundation Operants](https://www.bfskinner.org/wp-content/uploads/2016/02/OPERANTS_1_2016.pdf)).

The strongest building-level production reference is **Boylston Hall / Psychological Laboratory animal-work space**, not a fully specified Crozier room. The Harvard Crimson’s 1956 lab-history article says Emerson Hall was headquarters of the Psychological Laboratory from 1906 until 1946, while Boylston Hall’s fourth floor and attic were taken over in 1929 for elementary experimental psychology instruction and work with animals ([Harvard Crimson 1956](https://www.thecrimson.com/article/1956/12/20/psychological-labs-test-human-actions-in/)). The Skinner Foundation’s archive-based “Operants” issue adds that Crozier suggested Skinner thank the “management of the Boylston Hall Laboratory” for assistance in June 1931, and that Skinner listed “Boylston Hall” as a thereafter address in a June 1931 letter, making Boylston the strongest direct location lead for apparatus/animal-work production design ([B. F. Skinner Foundation Operants](https://www.bfskinner.org/wp-content/uploads/2016/02/OPERANTS_1_2016.pdf)).

Emerson Hall remains relevant as the pre-1946 Psychological Laboratory headquarters, but it is weaker as the specific visual target for the mature lever-box rat work unless a new source ties Skinner’s apparatus room to Emerson after 1929. Crozier and Hudson Hoagland remain essential as Biology/General Physiology influences, because a recent scholarly article notes that Skinner turned to Crozier and Hoagland in the Department of Biology after finding Harvard psychology insufficiently behavioristic, and that Crozier and Hoagland’s physiology courses discussed Pavlov and conditioned reflexes ([Verhaegh 2020](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8246940/)). 

Production-design wording should therefore be: **Harvard Psychological Laboratory / Boylston Hall animal-work and apparatus-building context, intellectually and administratively crossing into Crozier’s General Physiology after the 1931 NRC fellowship**. Do not label the scene simply “Emerson Hall psychology lab” or “Crozier’s physiology lab” unless future archive work identifies a precise room, building, or photograph.

The lab should look like an improvised instrument-making environment, not a polished medical laboratory. Bjork describes the Emerson Hall workshop with a circular saw, drill press, lathe, small milling machine, vise, hand tools, tins of small parts, screws, nuts, rivets, cotter keys, pins, piano wire, strap brass, plate brass, and steel ([Bjork 1993](https://gwern.net/doc/psychology/writing/1993-bjork-skinneralife.pdf)). Skinner’s 1956 autobiographical account reinforces that handmade culture through compressed-air releases, piano wires, cloth-covered platforms, light-wood runways, vertical glass plates, wooden disks, strings, solenoids, kymographs, and a custom brass pill machine ([Skinner 1956](https://www.appstate.edu/~steelekm/classes/psy5300/Documents/Skinner1956.pdf)).

No primary source located so far gives Skinner’s exact room number, window layout, room dimensions, or lighting fixture type for the 1928-1936 lab. Do not specify fluorescent-lit Memorial Hall for this sequence, because the Harvard Crimson’s fluorescent-lighted Memorial Hall psychology-lab description belongs to the 1946 postwar conversion, not Skinner’s early-1930s rat work ([Harvard Crimson 1946](https://www.thecrimson.com/article/1946/11/16/new-psychology-lab-stirs-aging-mem/)).

### Original apparatus

The original Skinner box did not begin as a polished commercial device. It evolved through a sequence of hand-built apparatuses including a compressed-air silent release box, a soundproofed box with a dark tunnel and observation window, a cloth-covered piano-wire platform, an eight-foot light-wood U-girder runway, a tilting runway with a wooden disk food dispenser, a covered tray, a solenoid-driven cumulative curve, and eventually a lever-equipped chamber ([Skinner 1956](https://www.appstate.edu/~steelekm/classes/psy5300/Documents/Skinner1956.pdf)). Skinner later wrote that he built “four soundproofed ventilated boxes, each containing a lever and a food magazine and supplied with a cumulative recorder,” which is the key primary statement for the mature Harvard-period box system ([Skinner 1956](https://www.appstate.edu/~steelekm/classes/psy5300/Documents/Skinner1956.pdf)).

Skinner’s later consolidated apparatus description gives precise lever geometry: the operant was a downward press on a small lever made of one-eighth-inch brass rod, with the accessible horizontal section 8 cm long, parallel to the wall, about 1 cm from the wall, and 8 to 10 cm above the floor ([Skinner 1938](https://www.bfskinner.org/wp-content/uploads/2016/02/BoO.pdf)). The rat had to press with about 10 g of pressure through about 1.5 cm of vertical movement, which supports a small practical lever rather than a large modern paddle ([Skinner 1938](https://www.bfskinner.org/wp-content/uploads/2016/02/BoO.pdf)).

The food mechanism evolved from pearl barley in an early tilting-runway system to disk-fed pellets and a later magnetic magazine. Skinner said pearl barley was used early because it was available in reasonably uniform pieces, and Vargas’s notebook analysis describes the first lever-linked arrangement as a disk that turned and dropped a pellet through a tube into a dish ([Skinner 1956](https://www.appstate.edu/~steelekm/classes/psy5300/Documents/Skinner1956.pdf), [Vargas 2004](https://www.bfskinner.org/wp-content/uploads/2015/04/Continegncies_Vargas_2004.pdf)). Skinner made pellets with a druggist-style pill machine using a fluted brass bed and cutter from stiff MacCollum-formula paste, which is a strong historically grounded prop for the lab bench ([Skinner 1956](https://www.appstate.edu/~steelekm/classes/psy5300/Documents/Skinner1956.pdf)).

### Recorder

The 1930s recording system should look kymograph-derived and hand-built, not like a later commercial chart recorder. Lattal describes the cumulative recorder’s inception in the 1930s through Skinner’s modified kymographs, and Asano and Lattal explain that Skinner’s early terminology was “rate recorder” or “summarizer,” while C. B. Ferster later named the device the “cumulative recorder” and the product the “cumulative record” ([Lattal 2004](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1285015/), [Asano and Lattal 2012](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3449858/)).

The physical principle for the sequence is simple: a response closes an electrical circuit; electromechanical parts step a pen upward; paper moves steadily with time. Skinner’s 1956 account states that each movement of the covered tray operated a solenoid to move a pen one step in a cumulative curve, and his later lever arrangement closed a switch operating a magnetic magazine ([Skinner 1956](https://www.appstate.edu/~steelekm/classes/psy5300/Documents/Skinner1956.pdf)). The cumulative record plots time horizontally and responses cumulatively upward, so a steeper slope means faster responding ([B. F. Skinner Foundation Cumulative Record](https://www.bfskinner.org/wp-content/uploads/2015/02/CUMULATIVE_RECORD.pdf)).

Do not use an Esterline-Angus paper-tape recorder for 1930-1936 Harvard. The documented technology is kymograph-derived, handmade, and Skinner-built, while later commercial recorders belong to Ferster, Gerbrands, and other instrument makers ([Lattal 2004](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1285015/), [Asano and Lattal 2012](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3449858/)). The Smithsonian’s Gerbrands cumulative recorder is useful as a later commercial reference, but it is not documented as a 1930-1936 Harvard device ([Smithsonian NMAH](https://americanhistory.si.edu/collections/nmah_690071)).

## Version B shooting script

### Runtime target

2:05 to 2:20.

### Visual style

- **Harvard timeline**: warm monochrome or desaturated sepia-color, tactile, analog, quiet.
- **Modern timeline**: cold black-and-white or desaturated blue-gray, glass, glow, repetition, isolation.
- **Cutting logic**: do not reveal the modern phone world until the Skinner loop is legible.

**BLACK.**

**CUT TO: EXTREME MACRO, PAPER**

A stylus scratches a thin line across moving paper. The line rises by one small step.

**SOUND:** paper scratch, faint relay tick.

**VO**

**INT. HARVARD LAB, CLOSE ON APPARATUS, EARLY 1930s**

Rat whiskers enter frame. A paw finds a simple metal lever. The lever depresses.

**SOUND:** lever click.

**CUT TO:** a pellet drops into a small food cup.

**SOUND:** pellet tick.

**CUT TO:** the recorder line rises again.

**VO**

> A movement.  
> A consequence.  
> A mark.

Young Skinner, slim and clean-shaven, sits close to the apparatus. He is not in a lab coat. He wears restrained academic clothing: dark jacket or shirt and tie.

The camera is too close. His face is still. The apparatus behind him is blurred.

**SOUND:** scratch, click, pellet, breath.

**VO**

> In a Harvard room, B.F. Skinner built a machine to remove the experimenter from the experiment.

**SUBTITLE OPTION**

> HARVARD UNIVERSITY  
> EARLY 1930s

Wood grain. Metal lever. Wire wound around a terminal. Relay armature. Food magazine. Paper roll. A screw tightened by hand.

**SOUND:** each object has its own close mechanical sound.

**INT. HARVARD LAB, WIDER BUT STILL CLAUSTROPHOBIC**

The chamber, recorder, wires, and Skinner occupy the same cramped composition. The room is sparse, academic, and hand-built.

Skinner writes a note. Do not show the full sentence unless it is sourced.

**SUBTITLE OPTION**

> RESPONSE. CONSEQUENCE. RETURN.

#### 0:45-0:52

**MATCH CUT: LEVER TO THUMB**

The rat paw presses the lever.

Cut on the downstroke to a modern thumb tapping a fictional phone interface.

No logos. No recognizable app. A blank vertical feed advances.

**SOUND:** lever click becomes soft haptic tick.

**VO**

#### 0:52-1:05

**INTERCUT: HARVARD / MODERN**

Rat presses lever. Phone feed advances. Pellet drops. Notification dot appears on fictional interface. Recorder rises. Engagement curve rises, abstract and unlabeled. Skinner watches paper. A modern face watches glass.

> response,  
> consequence,

**MODERN SERIES: INFINITE SCROLL**

A thumb scrolls in bed. A thumb scrolls on a train. A thumb scrolls at a desk. A thumb scrolls in a classroom.

The interfaces remain fictional and minimal. The repetition is the point.

**SOUND:** scroll friction gradually joins the recorder scratch.

**VO**

> A small action, answered quickly enough to ask for another.

#### 1:18-1:29

**INT. HARVARD LAB, SKINNER PROFILE**

Skinner is now slightly out of focus. The apparatus behind him sharpens.

The machine visually overtakes the frame.

**VO**

**SUBTITLE OPTION**

> NOT THE PHONE.  
> THE PATTERN.

**QUOTE CARD SLOT B**

#### 1:29-1:41

**ABSTRACT BRIDGE: RECORDER TO FEED**

The cumulative recorder line fills frame. It becomes a modern graph. The graph becomes a scroll path. The scroll path becomes the original recorder line again.

No numbers. No fake data. No branded analytics dashboard.

**VO**

> Once behavior could be measured, it could be shaped.

#### 1:41-1:55

**CULTURAL HEARING MONTAGE**

Not a courtroom. Book pages. Lecture hall silhouette. Hands turning paper. A student underlining “behavior.” A modern phone glowing in a dark room.

**SOUND:** music falls away. Only paper, breath, and relay ghosts.

**VO**

> The question was never only whether the box worked.

#### 1:55-2:08

**RETURN TO THE ORIGINAL LINE**

The recorder line continues. It now feels less like data and more like a sentence being written.

The rat presses the lever once more.

A pellet drops.

Silence after impact.

**VO**

> A rat pressed a bar.  
> A pellet fell.  
> The line moved.

**BLACK.**

## Subtitle strategy

Subtitles should be short, severe, and factual:

- HARVARD UNIVERSITY / EARLY 1930s
- THE LINE
- RESPONSE. CONSEQUENCE. RETURN.
- THE LOOP
- NOT THE PHONE. THE PATTERN.
- THE BOX WAS WOOD AND WIRE. THE QUESTION WAS HUMAN.

Avoid over-explaining with subtitles. They should feel like evidence tags or conceptual pressure points, not lecture slides.

## Quote-card strategy

Use no more than two quote cards in the first rough cut. Quote cards must be exact, sourced, and logged.

### Quote Card A: Experimental method

**Target placement:** 0:34-0:45, after “Behavior, recorded as it happened.”

**Purpose:** show Skinner’s own language about behavior, rate, recording, or experimental method.

**Allowed format:** on-screen quote card or narrator-read quote over apparatus.

**Required before use:** exact quotation, source title, author, year, page number or archive call number, URL or archive location if available, rights status.

### Quote Card B: Consequence or environment

**Target placement:** 1:18-1:35, after “Skinner was not building a phone.”

**Purpose:** connect Skinner’s own language to the broader question without implying he predicted phones or infinite scroll.

**Required warning:** do not use this quote to imply Skinner designed or foresaw infinite scroll unless the quote explicitly supports that claim.
